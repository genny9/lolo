Leather Crafting Tools

Before starting your first project, it is good to have a basic understanding of the tools are commonly used when dealing with leather. The tools for leather working are relatively inexpensive, long-lasting, and versatile for many different projects.

The most basic projects will need the following:Knife, glue, needle and thread, diamond chisels, a hammer, and a ruler.Chances are that whatever you're making will need a few additions to that list.

Check out this list of basic leather working tools to know what you need, and to understand what leather working skills require which tool.

FOR CUTTING & SKIVING

The round knife is the iconic leather working tool because of it's versatility when cutting and it's ability to skive. This is a great tool to have, but it takes some time to develop a skill for it and it requires good amount of sharpening.

FOR CUTTING

The rotary cutter is a great tool for beginners as it is easy to learn, cheap, and doesn't require any skills in sharpening. The blades can be sharpened by a rotary sharpener that is easy to use. They're also cheap and replaceable if you damage one.

FOR ROUNDING OUT EDGES

Edge bevelers have a sharpened notch that is used for removing part of your edges, helping to round them out. Having edges fold over is a common problem when edge bevelers are not used.

FOR WIDENING & CREATING HOLES

The stitching awl has a blade that gets wider approaching the base. The further the blade is pushed into the leather the larger the hole gets,  adjusting the size of a hole that was too small for the stitching. An awl is used while stitching to create each hole.

FOR STITCHING

Leather stitching needles are usually dull. This is because holes are made with an awl or diamond chisels. A certain kind of knot is used to attach the string to the needle prior to stitching. For this knot to hold, make sure that the eye of the needle is not too big nor too small for the size of the thread.

FOR HOLDING LEATHER

While a stitching horse is not completely necessary to stitch, it goes a long way in making it much easier. Leather is placed between the two wooden planks and then clamped into place. Once clamped, the horse holds the leather at a good height to stitch while you're sitting down.

FOR PUNCHING HOLES

Diamond chisels are used to punch stitching holes all the way through the leather. You may also need to use an awl as you're stitching to open up the holes. They come in a range of hole sizes and width, and are generally cheaper because they are meant to be replaced every so often.

FOR DIGGING A CHANNEL

By cutting a channel out of the leather, the stitching no longer sticks and therefore receives less friction in daily use. The line it creates is also serves as a guide when punching holes with a chisel. The groover on the left has interchangeable parts and also serves as a creaser - which creates decorative lines.

FOR SLICKING EDGES

Using friction, the edge burnisher melts fibers on the edge of the leather together, creating a slick surface that won't fray. 
The elongated side of the edge burnisher can be used with gum tragacanth to mat down the fibers on the underside of leather.

Punches are use to punch rivet holes, and they also come in a number of different sizes depending on what size hole you need.Most times, these holes are used for rivets or for the tongue of a belt buckle.

FOR HAMMERING

The hammer is used in leatherwork when using punches, rivet setters, and tooling. Using a rubber hammer is suggested to keep your punches and setters in good shape. A rubber hammer can be used to tool and attach parts together.

When using a one sided domed rivet, a domed setting rod is needed. If not used the rivets will end up looking smashed after the setting process. This will prevent either sides from getting smashed during the setting process.
